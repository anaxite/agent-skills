# Checklists

Quick-scan reference distilled from the Microsoft Writing Style Guide checklists.

## Acronyms

- Always spell out Microsoft product and feature names.
- Only use acronyms your audience is familiar with.
- Spell out the term first with the acronym in parentheses; use the acronym alone on subsequent mentions.
- Lowercase all words in the spelled-out form except proper nouns.
- Don't spell out acronyms listed in Merriam-Webster or marked as "use without spelling out" in the A–Z word list.
- Don't introduce an acronym used only once (unless needed for SEO).
- Avoid first use of an acronym in a title or heading (unless needed for SEO).
- Use *a* or *an* based on pronunciation (*an ISP*, *a SQL database*).
- Pluralize normally (*three APIs*).
- Avoid possessive form unless the acronym refers to a person or organization.
- In machine-translated content, watch for acronyms that form common English words (e.g., *RAM*).

## Capitalization

- Use sentence-style capitalization by default.
- Always capitalize proper nouns.
- Lowercase everything except the first word in sentences, headings, titles, UI labels, and phrases.
- Don't capitalize the spelled-out form of an acronym unless it's a proper noun.
- Use title-style capitalization for product/service names, book/song titles, article titles in citations, blog names, and titles of people.
- Follow traditional capitalization of keywords in programming languages.
- Don't use all uppercase for emphasis.
- Don't use all lowercase as a design choice.

## Grammar and parts of speech

- Use present-tense verbs (*is*, *open*). Avoid *will*, *was*, and *-ed* forms.
- Use indicative mood for statements of fact, imperative for procedures, subjunctive sparingly.
- Use active voice. Avoid passive voice where the receiver is the subject.
- Match verb to subject in person and number.
- Use second person (*you*) most of the time.
- Don't use gender-specific singular pronouns in generic references — use *you* or a role.
- Capitalize proper nouns; check Merriam-Webster or A–Z word list.
- Clarify ambiguous *-ing* words (e.g., *meeting requirements* — a meeting's requirements or how to meet them?).
- Avoid consecutive prepositional phrases — they're hard to read.
- Make sure modifiers clearly attach to the right word (*only the selected text is modified*, not *the selected text only is modified*).

## Numbers

### Spell out

- Zero through nine (unless space is limited).
- One of two adjacent numbers referring to different categories (*two 3-page articles*).
- Numbers at the beginning of a sentence.
- Ordinal numbers (*first*). Don't add *-ly* (*firstly*).

### Use numerals for

- Numbers in UI.
- 10 or greater.
- All numbers in a category if any one requires a numeral.
- Measurements (distance, temperature, volume, size, weight, pixels, points).
- Round numbers of 1 million or more.
- Numbers the customer types.
- Dimensions — use × (with spaces) for tiles/resolutions/paper; spell out *by* otherwise.
- Time of day.
- Percentages — use % with the numeral.
- Table/worksheet coordinates and numbered document sections.

### Commas in numbers

- Use in numbers with four or more digits. Exception: years and baud rates need five+ digits.
- Don't use in page numbers, addresses, or decimal fractions.

### Dates

- Use numerals, not ordinal words (*June 1*, not *June first*).
- Always spell out the month name.

### Negative numbers

- Use a minus sign, not an en dash.

### Fractions and decimals

- Don't use slash notation for fractions (exception: equations in text).
- Hyphenate spelled-out fractions (*one-third*; but *three sixty-fourths*).
- Align decimals on the decimal point in tables.
- Include a leading zero in decimals less than one (*0.125*) unless the customer types the value.
- Use singular for quantities of one or less, plural for zero.

### Ranges

- Use *from* and *through* for ranges. Use en dash only in page ranges or tight spaces.
- Don't combine *from* with an en dash range (*from 10–15*).

### Abbreviations (K, M, B)

- Avoid in UI unless space is limited; spell out in body content.
- Capitalize *K*, *M*, *B*; no space between number and abbreviation.
- Use decimal form (*8.25M*) only to save space.

## Procedures and instructions

### Step-by-step instructions

- Use numbered steps.
- Write a complete sentence per step — capitalize first word, end with a period.
- Use imperative verb forms.
- Consider a heading that tells the customer what the procedure accomplishes.
- Keep it short — fit the whole procedure on one screen; combine simple same-place actions into one step.
- Include actions that complete the procedure (*OK*, *Apply*).
- Make clear where each action takes place (e.g., "On the **Design** tab, …").
- For single-step procedures, use a bullet instead of a number (or plain text).
- Abbreviate simple menu sequences with **>** (with spaces): **Accounts** > **Other accounts** > **Add an account**.

### UI interaction verbs

- *Open* — apps, shortcut menus, files, folders.
- *Close* — apps, panes, dialogs, windows, files, folders.
- *Leave* — websites and webpages.
- *Go to* — menus, tabs, ribbon locations.
- *Select* — UI options, values, links, menu items.
- *Select and hold* — press-and-hold for ~1 second.
- *Clear* — remove checkbox selection.
- *Choose* — general choice, not a specific selection.
- *Enter* — type or input a value.
- *Specify* — type or select a value in a combo box (technical audiences only).
- *Move* — relocate something (drag, paste, etc.).
- *Zoom / zoom in / zoom out* — change magnification.
- Avoid *press*, *press and hold*, and *right-click* — use input-neutral verbs.

### Alternative and multiple input methods

- Use input-neutral verbs by default.
- Always document keyboard procedures for accessibility.
- Be consistent when documenting mouse and keyboard together; don't combine with + (*hold down Shift while clicking*, not *Shift+click*).
- For touch/pen: use *tap*, *double-tap*, *tap and hold*, *pan*, *flick*, *swipe* — not *click*, *scroll*, or *drag*.
- For multiple methods, use a table with a column per input method, or document the primary method with alternatives in parentheses.

## Punctuation

- Keep sentences short and simple.
- End all sentences with a period.
- Use one space after terminal punctuation and colons.
- Place a colon at the end of a phrase that directly introduces a list.
  - If list items complete the introductory phrase, end each with a period.
  - If all items are short phrases (≤3 words), omit periods.
  - If any item is a complete sentence, end every item with a period.
- Use the Oxford comma (serial comma after every item, including the last).
- Use a comma after introductory phrases, between independent clauses joined by a conjunction, and around the year in a full date.
- Use apostrophes for contractions and noun possessives. Use *its* (not *it's*) for possessive.
- After a colon, lowercase the next word unless it's a proper noun or the start of a quotation.
- Rewrite sentences with semicolons as multiple sentences or a list.
- Use exclamation points sparingly.
- Use question marks sparingly — provide answers, don't ask.
- Place closing quotation marks outside commas and periods, inside other punctuation (unless the punctuation is part of the quoted material).
- Omit hyphens unless needed to prevent confusion.
- Don't use spaces around em dashes.
- Don't use a slash as a substitute for *or*.
- Format punctuation in the same font style as the surrounding text (unless it's part of a styled element like a command or link).

## Responsive content

- Plan for tablet and mobile viewing.
- Consider column display order on small screens.
- Write short sections and short paragraphs — a full paragraph should fit on one screen.
- Limit headings to one short line.
- Organize infographics in compact sections with an enlarge option.
- Simplify charts/graphs; write short titles and axis labels.
- Limit table columns.

## Text formatting

- Use sentence-style capitalization (except proper nouns).
- Use left alignment — never center or justify.
- Avoid widows, orphans, and lines ending with hyphens.
- Don't compress line spacing.
- Format text elements consistently.

## Word choice

- Use common contractions (*that's*, *don't*).
- Avoid words with multiple meanings.
- Omit unnecessary adverbs; use precise one-word verbs (*try*, not *attempt to*).
- Use the same word for the same concept; check the A–Z word list.
- Don't repurpose common words with technical meanings (*bucket* for *group*).
- Don't use nouns as verbs or verbs as nouns.
- Don't coin new words when existing ones work.
- Define technical terms in context; prefer plain language.
- Use your audience's terminology; consult industry glossaries.
- Avoid jargon when a familiar word exists.
- Don't attribute human characteristics to devices or products.
