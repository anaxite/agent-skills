# Global-ready content

Rules for writing content that works worldwide and supports localization and machine translation.

## Art and images

- Choose simple, generic images appropriate worldwide (landscapes, highway signs, soccer, pens).
- Avoid holiday, seasonal, or religious imagery.
- Avoid major landmarks and well-known buildings — they may have legal protections or political/religious associations.
- Avoid hand signs and art based on English idioms.
- Avoid social or work situations involving men and women together — risky in some locales.
- Choose colors carefully — colors may carry religious, cultural, or political meaning (especially flag colors). Prefer neutral or brand colors.
- Keep text out of graphics when possible; machine translation cannot process embedded text. Use captions instead.
- If text must appear in graphics, make it easy to edit. Store art in separate files, not embedded in documents.
- Add alt text to all meaningful images. Decorative images don't need alt text.
- Check import restrictions on content (especially maps) for target countries/regions.

## Currency

- Lowercase currency names; capitalize the country/region reference.
  - ✓ *US dollar*, *Brazilian real*, *South African rand*
  - ✗ *us Dollar*, *brazilian Real*
- In structured lists or tables, capitalizing currency names is acceptable.
- For specific amounts, use the ISO currency code followed by the amount with no space.
  - ✓ *BRL2.89 billion (USD1.42 billion)*
  - ✗ *2.89 billion BRL*
- When the currency is unambiguous, the symbol alone is acceptable.
  - ✓ *$1.42 billion*, *€1.42 billion*
- Use the euro symbol (€), not the word *euros*, with specific amounts.

## Examples and scenarios

- Be sensitive to cultural perceptions — social situations, politics, religion, holidays, sports, traditions, and legal practices vary worldwide.
- Avoid mentioning real places, or use recognizable cities from different parts of the world and vary them across examples.
- Avoid discussing technologies or standards that aren't used worldwide (phone, video, measurement, paper size, character sets, text direction).
- Don't assume US standards are familiar or relevant to all readers.

## Names and contact information

### Form fields

- Use *First name* and *Last name*, or simply *Full name*.
- Make *Middle name* optional if included.
- Use *Title* (not *Honorific*) for fields like *Mr.* or *Mrs.*
- Use *State or province* instead of *State*. Make it optional.
- Use *Country or region* instead of *Country* (to accommodate disputed territories). *Country/Region* is acceptable when space is limited.
- Use *Postal code* instead of *ZIP Code*. Allow at least 10 characters, letters and numbers.
- Provide fields long enough for all locales (addresses, phone numbers).

### Addressing users by name

- Don't assume it's appropriate to address customers by name — some markets and cultures consider it inappropriate.
- Confirm the app can detect the customer's region and use the correct form of address.

## Time and place

### Dates

- Begin calendars on Monday (customary in much of the world).
- Use the format *month dd, yyyy* (e.g., *January 5, 2018*).
- Don't use numerals to represent months.

### Time

- Include the time zone when it matters (e.g., event listings).
- Omit the time zone in timestamps that auto-display the customer's local time.

### Seasons

- Avoid referring to seasons. Use months or calendar quarters instead.
- If a season must be mentioned, specify the hemisphere.

### Places

- Include the country or region name in event locations.

## Web, software, and HTML considerations

### Browser and experience support

- Support browsers likely used by worldwide audiences, including older versions.
- Design content so fonts are substituted if the specified font is unavailable.
- Avoid hand-drawn fonts and hard-coded fonts in text or code.
- Use standard HTML tags, not proprietary tags.
- Globalize text generated by scripts.

### Download time

- Keep pages small; provide text-only versions for larger content.
- Design pages so text loads first, graphics second.

### Multiple languages

- Support right-to-left and top-to-bottom reading directions.
- Place key information (e.g., home page links) at both upper-right and upper-left.
- Allow space for text expansion due to localization.

### Legal and availability

- Follow US and local laws on software distribution and downloads.
- Verify that downloads comply with US export law and are legal in the target country/region.
- Verify product and format availability — localized versions may lag behind US releases.

### Links

- Link only to sites available and useful worldwide.
- Prefer linking to sites where readers can select their country/region.
- Flag links and cross-references that aren't globally relevant; inform readers when linking to non-globalized content.

## Writing tips for global content

### Sentence structure

- Write short, simple sentences. Multiple commas usually signal a complex sentence — rewrite or split it.
- Replace complex sentences and paragraphs with lists and tables.
- Use active voice and indicative mood most of the time; imperative mood in procedures.
- Avoid linking more than two (ideally) or three phrases with *and*, *or*, or *but*.

### Clarity helpers

- Include *that* and *who* to clarify sentence structure.
  - ✓ *Verify that all tables were migrated.*
  - ✗ *Verify all tables were migrated.*
- Include articles (*a*, *an*, *the*) to help readers and translation software parse nouns and modifiers.
  - ✓ *Empty the container.* / *The empty container*
- Include verbs in short headings and labels when needed for clarity.
  - ✓ *Access is denied*
  - ✗ *Access denied*
- Keep adjectives and adverbs close to the words they modify. Watch placement of *only*.
- Avoid modifier stacks (long chains of modifying words).
  - ✗ *an extremely well thought-out Windows migration project plan*
  - ✓ *a project plan that's well thought out*

### Vocabulary

- Use sentence-style capitalization. Capitalize only proper nouns, trademarks, and product names.
- Avoid idioms, colloquial expressions, and culture-specific references.
- Use one word for one concept consistently — don't alternate synonyms.
- Don't reuse the same word for different concepts.
- Use only common, well-established abbreviations (e.g., *USB*). Check with localization if uncertain.

### Machine translation tips

- Use conventional grammar and punctuation; balance friendly voice with clear English.
- Use standard word order: subject + verb + object.
- Limit sentence fragments — they are hard to translate.
- Clarify *-ing* words (can be verb, adjective, or noun). Use sentence structure or helper words.
- Clarify *-ed* words (can be modifier or part of verb phrase):
  - Add a determiner: *They have **an** added functionality.*
  - Add a form of *be*: *limits **that are based** on storage space*
  - Split the sentence or rewrite to remove the ambiguous *-ed* word.
