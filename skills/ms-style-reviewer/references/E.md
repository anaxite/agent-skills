## email

Use to refer generically to an electronic mail program, to refer collectively to email messages, or to refer to individual email messages. If necessary to distinguish these meanings, use *email message* or *message* to refer to an individual piece of email. Make sure it's clear that you're not referring to instant messaging. **Global tip** In some languages, the translation of *email* is different for noun and verb forms. **Examples** You have two new emails. Scroll through email to find the message you want to read. You have new mail. Email us your comments.

## emoji, emoticons

An *emoji* (plural *emoji*) is a small image that represents an emotion, concept, or object. Examples include 😀 and ☹️.

An *emoticon* is a combination of keyboard characters that expresses an emotion or attitude. Examples include :) for a smiling face and :( for a frowning face.

In social media posts and informal communications, emoji and emoticons add personality and can help communicate meaning and tone.

Use emoji instead of emoticons. Emoji are better for accessibility because:

- Every emoji has a type of alt text that screen readers can read out to users.
- Screen readers don't interpret the combination of characters in an emoticon. Instead, they try to process the characters literally, ignore them, or indicate their presence only through a pause.

However, not all screen readers support emoji. Always ensure that your intended meaning and tone get across without them.

## General guidelines for emoji use

Follow these guidelines to use emoji in a way that's effective, positive, and accessible:

- Don't use emoji in content that's related to a serious topic. They might make the topic seem unserious or trivial.
- Don't use emoji in place of words.
- Don't use emoji in the middle of a sentence. Put them at the end of the sentence. Even better, put them at the end of the paragraph, message, or post.
- To keep emoji from losing their impact, don't overuse them.

    A general rule is to use a maximum of three emoji per message or post. Use a maximum of three emoji in a row, and always put a space between them.
- Don't use emoji at the beginning of consecutive lines to simulate bullets in a list.
- Don't embed emoji (or emoticons) in images.
- Don't use emoji in profile names.

## Guidelines for choosing emoji

- **Limit yourself to popular emoji that are widely recognized and understood**. Avoid ambiguous and obscure emoji.
- **Ensure that the literal and figurative meanings of an emoji reflect your intended meaning**. The literal meaning of every emoji is provided by a string that's called the *Common Locale Data Repository (CLDR) short name* or *Unicode name*. Screen readers use this name as alt text.

    In addition to its literal meaning, an emoji might have one or more figurative meanings. Make sure that both types of meaning are consistent with what you're trying to convey.

    **Tip** You can find the CLDR short name for every emoji on sites such as [Unicode.org](https://unicode.org/emoji/charts/full-emoji-list.html) and [Emojipedia.org](https://emojipedia.org/).
- **Consider potential cultural and diversity implications, and avoid any emoji that might be inappropriate**. The connotations of an emoji can vary across cultures and communities. What one group considers innocent, friendly, or humorous might be offensive to another group. To learn more, see [Bias-free communication](/en-us/style-guide/bias-free-communication) and [Global communications](/en-us/style-guide/global-communications).

    **Note** Emoji (and emoticons) might be removed from localized versions of your content.
- **When you feel you can, use the standard version of emoji**. Custom emoji that represent different skin tones are great for inclusiveness, but they can have longer alt text and take longer for screen readers to read.
- **Use emoji that work across devices**. The appearance of an emoji can vary across platforms and devices. Websites such as [Emojipedia.org](https://emojipedia.org/) show the variations of each emoji. Avoid emoji that might appear to users in a form that changes or undermines your intended meaning.

## enable, enables

Avoid using in most cases.

Don’t use *enables* (or *allows* or *lets*) to refer to things that Microsoft or Microsoft apps make possible for the customer. Rewrite to focus on what’s important or possible from the customer’s point of view. For example, say, "Write, draw, and take notes on webpages with Microsoft Edge," and not "Microsoft Edge enables you to write, draw, and take notes on webpages."

In product UI, use *On/Off* (not *Enable/Disable*) as labels for a switch that controls the availability of a feature or a capability in the product. Or use more specific pairs of words such as *show/hide*, *add/remove*, or *allow/block*.

## euro

Lowercase.

Plural: *euros*

Symbol: €

In US content, place the euro symbol in front of the amount. Different locales might place the euro symbol elsewhere, as appropriate. A euro is divided into 100 *cents.* When expressing an amount in euros and cents in US content, use a decimal point. Different locales might use a decimal point or a comma, as appropriate.

**Example** €3.50

Use *supports the euro currency standard* to refer to a country's use of the euro.

Use the following phrases to refer to countries that have adopted the euro as their currency:

- European Union (EU) members trading in euros
- European Union (EU) members that have adopted the euro
- euro nations
- members of the Economic and Monetary Union (EMU)

Use references to the EMU cautiously. Some people aren't familiar with the organization.

**Note** On subsequent mentions, it's OK to use the abbreviations *EU* and *EMU.* It's also OK to refer to EU members as *EU member states* and to EMU members as *EMU member states.*

Use *non-euro nations* to refer to EU member states that haven't adopted the euro as their currency.

The terms *euroland* and *eurozone* are OK to use on websites with an informal tone. Don't use these terms in product documentation or other formal contexts, especially if the content will be localized.
