# Voice and Tone

> Distilled from: Microsoft's brand voice, Top 10 tips for Microsoft style and voice, Bias-free communication, Militaristic language.

## Brand voice principles

Microsoft voice is **warm and relaxed**, **crisp and clear**, and **ready to lend a hand**.

- Be natural, conversational, and grounded — occasionally fun
- Write for scanning first, reading second
- Anticipate real customer needs; offer the right information at the right time

## Top 10 style and voice tips

### 1. Use bigger ideas, fewer words
- Shorter is always better
- ✗ "If you're ready to purchase Office 365 for your organization, contact your Microsoft account representative."
- ✓ "Ready to buy? Contact us."

### 2. Write like you speak
- Read text aloud; avoid jargon and overly technical language
- ✗ "Invalid ID"
- ✓ "You need an ID that looks like this: someone@example.com"

### 3. Project friendliness with contractions
- Use contractions: *it's, you'll, you're, we're, let's*
- ✗ "what you are interested in, what is on your calendar"
- ✓ "what you're interested in, what's on your calendar"

### 4. Get to the point fast
- Lead with the most important information
- Front-load keywords for scanning
- Make choices and next steps obvious

### 5. Be brief
- Give just enough information for confident decisions
- Prune every excess word
- ✗ "The Recommended Charts command on the Insert tab recommends charts that are likely to represent your data well. Use the command when you want to visually present data, and you're not sure how to do it."
- ✓ "Create a chart that's just right for your data by using the Recommended Charts command on the Insert tab."

### 6. Default to sentence-style capitalization
- Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns
- Never use title case
- ✗ "Find a Microsoft Partner" → ✓ "Find a Microsoft partner"
- ✗ "Limited-Time Offer" → ✓ "Limited-time offer"

### 7. Skip end punctuation on headings and short list items
- No periods on titles, headings, subheadings, UI titles, or list items ≤ 3 words
- Save periods for body copy and paragraphs

### 8. Use the Oxford comma
- In a list of three or more items, include a comma before the conjunction
- ✗ "Android, iOS and Windows"
- ✓ "Android, iOS, and Windows"

### 9. One space after punctuation; no spaces around dashes
- One space after periods, question marks, and colons
- No spaces around em dashes
- ✗ "pipelines — logical groups — to consolidate"
- ✓ "pipelines—logical groups—to consolidate"

### 10. Revise weak writing
- Start statements with a verb
- Edit out *you can* and *there is / there are / there were*
- ✗ "You can access Office apps across your devices, and you get online file storage and sharing."
- ✓ "Store files online, access them from all your devices, and share them with coworkers."

## Bias-free communication

### Gender-neutral language
- Use gender-neutral alternatives for common terms:

| ✓ Use | ✗ Avoid |
|---|---|
| chair, moderator | chairman |
| humanity, people, humankind | man, mankind |
| operates, staffs | mans |
| sales representative | salesman |
| synthetic, manufactured | manmade |
| workforce, staff, personnel | manpower |

### Pronouns
- Avoid *he, him, his, she, her, hers* in generic references
- Rewrite to use second person (*you*), plural nouns, articles (*the, a*), or role nouns (*reader, customer*)
- Singular *they/their/them* is acceptable when you can't write around it
- Never use *he/she* or *s/he*
- Use a real person's preferred pronouns when writing about them

### Diversity and inclusion
- In fictitious scenarios, use diverse names and avoid stereotyped job roles
- Represent diverse perspectives in text and images (gender identity, race, culture, ability, age, sexual orientation, socioeconomic class)
- Avoid examples that reflect primarily Western or affluent lifestyles
- Don't generalize about people, countries, regions, or cultures
- Don't use slang that could be cultural appropriation (e.g., *spirit animal*)
- Don't use profane or derogatory terms (e.g., *pimp, bitch*)

### Unconscious-bias terms

| ✓ Use | ✗ Avoid |
|---|---|
| primary/subordinate | master/slave |
| perimeter network | demilitarized zone (DMZ) |
| stop responding | hang |

### Disability language
- Focus on people, not disabilities ("readers who are blind," not "blind readers")
- Avoid pity language (*stricken with, suffering from*)
- Don't mention disability unless it's relevant

### Race and ethnicity
- Title-case: Asian, Black, African American, Hispanic, Latinx, Native American, Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian, Pacific Islander, Indigenous Peoples
- Lowercase: multiracial, white

## Militaristic language

Avoid terms associated with violence and military actions unless referring to physical combat operations.

### Cybersecurity term replacements

| ✓ Use | ✗ Avoid |
|---|---|
| address; protect against; respond to | combat; fight; eliminate |
| cyberattack chain | (cyber) kill chain |
| cyberattacker; bad actor; threat actor | attacker; adversary |
| impact | blast radius |
| multilayered approach; defense-in-depth cybersecurity | defense-in-depth approach |
| protect; safeguard; defend | guard; ward |
| secured | locked down |
| security; protection; defense | fortifications; first line of defense; frontlines |
| security teams; security analysts; defenders | frontline analysts |
| vulnerabilities; points of access; external exposure | external attack surface |

### Conditional usage
- **attack** — OK with a preceding descriptor (e.g., *malware attacks*); otherwise write *cyberattack* (one word, no hyphen)
- **defend / defense / defenses** — OK when cybersecurity context is clear in the same sentence
- **threat** — OK with a preceding descriptor; otherwise write *cyberthreat* (one word, no hyphen)
- **threat intelligence** — OK when cybersecurity context is clear; never shorten to *threat intel*

### Never use in cybersecurity context
- air cover, bomb, email bomb, mail bomb, time bomb
- enemy, enemies, enemy lines
- go on the offensive
- invade, invasion
- missile, torpedo
- nuke, go nuclear
- strike, troops
